In this day and age, computers are everywhere. One would be hard-pressed to find a
sphere of human life that does not involve computers today. Considering the capabilities of the
modern computers that we see all around us, it would not be a stretch to believe that computers
can do pretty much everything given enough processing power. However, that is actually not
the case. Let us consider the questions, “what can computers do?” and “what can computers
not do?”
These questions can actually be interpreted in two different ways. On one hand, we
could consider what computers can do in principle, and whether there are things that they
simply cannot do, even with unlimited computational power. And if there do exist such things,
then what are they? As it turns out, there actually are problems that a computer cannot solve,
no matter how much computational power it might have.

theoretical computer science known as computability theory.
On the other hand, we could also interpret these questions this way: what can computers
do in practice? Are there things they can do in principle, but which have such high

to determine whether they are practically solvable or not is the area of complexity theory.
Introduction to Complexity Theory
Let us leave the question of computability aside, and focus on complexity. Say we have
a list of problems that can be computed (i.e., they can be solved by a computer). What we need
is a way to classify them according to the amount of computational resources required to solve
them, in order to determine whether it is actually practical to do so or not. This is where
complexity classes come in. Complexity classes are basically categories of problems that have
been defined on the basis of their computational requirements.
Note that when we talk about computational resources, we are actually talking about
time and space, and that too in a rather abstract manner. While at first glance, it might seem
that quantities like processor clock cycles, number of cores, gigabytes of memory etc. would
be more appropriate for being considered as computational resources, it would actually not be
a good idea to use concrete, physical quantities for this purpose as that would restrict our
classification to particular machines, architectures, operating systems etc.
As it happens, all these requirements ultimately boil down to two things. The quantities
that together make up the ‘processing power’ of a computer actually determine the time it takes
to perform a given task. And the ‘memory’ of the computer actually just determines the space
it takes to store data for performing the given task. So, what we do is that we interpret
computational power in an abstract manner in terms of time and space only. Though, once
again, in the interests of keeping our analysis as generally applicable as possible, we do not
use typical units for these quantities, and instead consider them in terms of the size of the input.

algorithm used to solve that problem as the complexity of the problem itself, because what if
the algorithm in question were to be needlessly complicated? So, what we do is that we

‘most efficient’). So, the complexity of a problem is defined by the lower bound on the
complexity of any algorithm that solves it, though proving these lower bounds can often be
difficult.

Classical Complexity Classes
There is, in fact, a very large number of complexity classes that have been defined to
this day, and new ones keep getting defined as time goes on and new discoveries are made.
One excellent resource for keeping track of complexity classes is an online platform called
Complexity Zoo, which currently contains over four hundred complexity classes. Since it will
not be possible for us to discuss more than a few of them in the course of this article, we shall
focus only on the very important ones which come up the most during discussions on
complexity theory.
However, before we move forward with the descriptions of those classes, there is one
important point that needs to be mentioned. Just as we used abstract notions of time and space
instead of concrete quantities like processing power and computer memory while dealing with
algorithm complexities, we also use an abstract model of a computer instead of a particular
physical implementation. This abstract model is the Turing machine, and in simple words, it
has the property that it can do whatever any actual computer can. Alternatively, no computer
can do what a Turing machine cannot do. Therefore, it is a good model to use in place of any
kind of computer for the purpose of studying complexities. Turing machines can be
deterministic, non-deterministic or probabilistic. These terms will come up in our descriptions
of various complexity classes, which we shall now move on to.
The class P (polynomial time) contains all decision problems that can be solved by a
deterministic Turing machine (or DTM) in polynomial time. Let us explore the technical terms
we just encountered. A deterministic Turing machine is the most basic Turing machine, which
uses a fixed set of rules to determine its future actions, while a decision problem is a
computational problem that can be posed as a yes/no question. For example, primality testing
is a decision problem, because for any given number, you can ask the question, “Is the given
number prime?” and this question has only two possible answers: yes, or no. On the other hand,
prime factorisation is not fundamentally a decision problem, because it requires answering
with a list of prime factors of the given number, not just a yes or no. However, it can be recast
as a series of decision problems of the form "Does the given number have a prime factor less
than or equal to ?" for various values of . Therefore, while it might seem that such a function
problem (i.e. one which expects an output that is not just yes or no)., or any other type of
problem (such as an optimisation problem) might need to be dealt with differently, we can
actually recast such problems into decision problems as well, so we will continue to talk only
about decision problems in our discussion. Anyways, the problems in P (such as Primality
Testing and Maximum Cardinality Matching) are the ones that are typically considered to be
tractable, or ‘efficiently solvable.’
The class NP (nondeterministic polynomial time), on the other hand, can be defined in
two different ways, which are both fundamentally equivalent. One definition is that it contains
all decision problems that can be solved by a nondeterministic Turing machine (or NTM) in
polynomial time. A non-deterministic Turing machine is a Turing machine with an added
feature of non-determinism, which allows it to have multiple possible future actions from a
given state. One way to view non-determinism is that the Turing machine branches into many
possible computational paths at each step, and if it solves the problem in any of these branches,
it is said to have solved the problem. Now, if you have studied automata theory, you might be
aware that NTMs are not actually any more powerful than DTMs due to the fact that any NTM
can be simulated using a DTM too. So, how does this definition make NP any different from
P? The thing to note here is that the equivalence of NTMs and DTMs is with regard to
computability. A DTM can do anything that an NTM can do; however, it cannot necessarily do
it with the same time complexity, due to the overhead associated with simulating one machine
using another. Thus, NP does, in fact, constitute a broader set of problems than P.
The second way of defining the class NP is that it contains all decision problems whose
solutions can be verified by a deterministic Turing machine in polynomial time. Note that here
we are not talking about solving a problem, but instead checking whether a given solution to
that problem is correct or not. Intuition suggests that this is something that can be done much
more efficiently compared with solving a problem from scratch. Take prime factorisation as an
example. If you are given a number and asked to find its prime factors, it will be difficult (at
least with the algorithms we currently have for this task). However, if you are given a number
as well as a list of primes that are claimed to be its prime factors, it will be very easy to verify
whether that is the case or not. So, problems in NP (such as the Boolean Satisfiability Problem
and the Hamiltonian Path Problem) can be solved using an NTM, and verified using a DTM,
both in polynomial time.
At this point, it can be noted that the class P is clearly a subset of NP. After all, if you
can simply solve a problem in polynomial time using a DTM, then you can also verify a given
solution by comparison with the solution you obtained by solving the problem yourself.
However, whether it is a proper subset of NP is open to debate. It is known that all problems
in P are also in NP. But it is not known whether all problems in NP are also in P, or there are
problems in NP that are not in P. This is known as the P versus NP problem and is considered
to be the most important open question in all of computer science. As a matter of fact, it even
has a $1 million prize on it, which should tell you how important the study of complexity
classes is, and encourage you to study this article with more interest . To be clear, the prize
is for providing a proof of either case, whether P = NP or P  NP. However, it is widely believed
that P  NP, which matches the intuition we discussed earlier as well. After all, it seems only
natural for solving a problem to be more difficult than verifying a given solution to it for some
problems, right? Despite this, the alternative is not completely out of the question yet, though
it will have enormous implications for our understanding of computer science if it does
somehow turn out to be the case, because it would mean that every problem can be solved as
easily as a given solution for it can be checked.
In any case, another complexity class is NP-hard. A problem is NP-hard if any problem
in NP can be reduced to it in polynomial time. Reduction is a concept in complexity theory
which involves transforming one problem into another to show that the second problem is at
least as difficult as the first, or that the first is no more difficult than the second. For instance,
if an algorithm for solving problem B efficiently (if it existed) could also be used as a
subroutine to solve problem A efficiently, we would say that problem A is reducible to problem
B and it would mean that solving A would not be harder than solving B. What this means for
us is that an NP-hard problem (such as Subset Sum) is at least as hard as the hardest problems
in NP, and that finding a polynomial time algorithm to solve a single NP-hard problem would
give polynomial time algorithms for all the problems in NP. Keep in mind, however, that an
NP-hard problem does not necessarily have to be in NP itself.
The class NP-complete can be thought of as an intersection of both of the above
classes: if a problem is in both NP and NP-hard, it is NP-complete. What this means is that
NP-complete problems are the hardest problems in NP and if a polynomial time solution is
found for any NP-complete problem, it would be equivalent to solving all problems in NP
because they can all be reduced to the NP-complete problem (as it is also NP-hard). The P
versus NP problem is a very important consideration here. If P = NP, i.e. all problems that can
be verified in polynomial time using a DTM can also be solved in polynomial time using a
DTM, then the classes P, NP and NP-complete contain the same problems and are effectively
the same class, though, of course, this is very unlikely as it is widely believed that P  NP, as
we discussed earlier.
Let us now consider incorporating randomness into our discussion. The class BPP
(bounded-error probabilistic polynomial time) consists of decision problems that can be solved
by a probabilistic Turing machine in polynomial time. This means that the TM can make
random choices during the course of its operation. As a result, the correct result is not
guaranteed. However, it must provide the correct answer with high probability, conventionally
taken to be 2/3. This means that the probability of error must be bounded by 1/3, thus its name.
Note that this number is arbitrary, and the bound can actually be tightened by running the
randomised algorithm multiple times due to which the probability of error will decrease. With
regard to the relation of BPP with the other classes, any problem that is in P is also in BPP,
because a deterministic Turing machine is just a specific case of a probabilistic Turing machine
with the probability of one choice being 1 and the rest 0 at each juncture. On the other hand,
whether any problem that is in BPP is also in P has not been proven, though it is conjectured
that this is the case (despite the existence of problems such as Polynomial Identity Testing that
are in BPP but not yet shown to be in P, because the number of such problems is decreasing
quickly). The relation of BPP with NP is also not yet clear.
Up till now, the classes we have been looking at are defined based on time complexity.
Let us have a look at an important class that is defined in terms of space complexity as well.
The class PSPACE contains all decision problems that can be solved using a polynomial
amount of memory, no matter how much time they take and whether they are solved by a
deterministic or nondeterministic Turing machine. All the classes we have discussed till now
are in PSPACE.
There are many other important classes in complexity theory as well, such as L
(logarithmic space), NL (nondeterministic logarithmic space), EXPTIME (exponential time),
EXPSPACE (exponential space) etc., but discussing them is outside of our scope. Instead, we
shall now move on to our primary topic—quantum complexity classes.
Quantum Complexity Classes
Quantum complexity theory is a subfield of complexity theory that deals with
complexity classes defined on the basis of quantum computing, which utilises principles of
quantum mechanics such as superposition, entanglement and interference to solve certain
problems much more efficiently than classical computers. While quantum computing is usually
studied in the context of the quantum circuit model, quantum complexity theory typically
defines classes using quantum Turing machines (which are, of course, equivalent to any other
model of quantum computing, just like a typical Turing machine is equivalent to any other
model of classical computing). In a quantum Turing machine, the states of the machine are
actually pure or mixed quantum states, while the transition function is replaced by a set of
unitary matrices. On another note, it should be noted that since quantum computing is still an
emerging field, the number of quantum complexity classes is not as extensive as their classical
counterparts. In particular, there are two main quantum complexity classes that are widely
discussed in literature on the topic.
The first important quantum complexity class to consider is BQP (bounded-error
quantum polynomial time). It is the class of decision problems solvable by a quantum computer
in polynomial time, where the probability of producing the correct answer is at least 2/3 (i.e.
with an error bounded by 1/3, similar to BPP, but using quantum algorithms). This class is
actually the quantum counterpart of the classical class BPP, with the number 1/3 again being
arbitrary and subject to change in case multiple runs of an algorithm are considered. However,
the exact relationship between BPP and BQP is not proven. It is known that BPP is a subset of
BQP, that is, any problem in BPP is also in BQP. However, it is not known whether every
problem in BQP is also in BPP, though it is widely believed that this is not the case, which
would show that quantum computers are fundamentally more powerful than classical
computers.
One point that appears to support this case is the demonstration of oracle separation
between BPP and BQP. In complexity theory, an oracle is an abstract machine used to study
relative computational power. It is taken to be a black box that can solve a particular problem
as a single operation. This is useful in the sense that we do not have to consider the inner
workings of the algorithm solving that problem, we just place a call to the oracle as part of
another algorithm, and we get the answer in a single step. Thus, we sometimes consider the
complexities of various classes with respect to the number of queries made to an oracle.
Quantum algorithms such as the Bernstein-Vazirani algorithm and Simon’s algorithm provide
oracle separation between BPP and BQP, which suggests that BQP and BPP are distinct classes.
In other words, there are problems solvable in BQP, as long as you have access to certain
oracles, that are not solvable in BPP, even with access to those oracles, implying that quantum
computers have capabilities beyond classical probabilistic machines. However, the relationship
between BPP and BQP without considering oracles remains an open problem.
With regard to the relationship between BQP and P, there are problems that are in BQP
such as integer factorisation and discrete logarithms, but for which no polynomial time
classical algorithm has yet been found, meaning that they are not in P. This suggests that P is a
proper subset of BQP, though it is not definitively proven yet. As for the relationship between
BQP and NP, not much is known except for the fact that there is some overlap between the two
classes (e.g. the NP problems of integer factorisation and discrete logarithms mentioned earlier
being in BQP as well). However, it is conjectured that not all NP problems are in BQP, which
means that there are efficiently checkable problems that are not efficiently solvable even by
quantum computers. Note that this is the same as claiming that the classes BQP and NP-
complete are disjoint, since if even a single NP-complete problem were to be in BQP, every
NP problem would be in BQP.
Let us take a step back at this point and recall what we have just discussed, as we went
through several points rather quickly. BPP is a subset of BQP. However, there is at least oracle
separation between BPP and BQP, although it is believed that there is a more general separation
as well. What this means is that any problem that can be solved with high probability using a
probabilistic (classical) Turing machine can also be solved with high probability using a
quantum Turing machine (i.e. a quantum computer). But there are problems that can be solved
by a quantum computer with the help of an oracle that cannot be solved by a classical computer
even with the help of the oracle, and it is likely that this relation holds true even without an
oracle involved, though this has not been proven yet. Moreover, while every problem in P is
also in BQP, it appears likely that not every problem in BQP is in P, as in that case, quantum
computers would not really be any more powerful than classical computers. Lastly, there is
some overlap between the classes BQP and NP, but neither appears to be a subset of the other.
Since that implies there being problems in NP that are not in BQP, it means that quantum
computers are not magical devices that can solve everything; instead, there are problems that
cannot be solved efficiently even with quantum computers.
The other important quantum complexity class is QMA (Quantum Merlin Arthur),
which is the quantum analogue of the classical class NP. Recall that in NP, a given solution to
a problem can be verified in polynomial time. Let us call the suggested solution a ‘proof’ or
‘certificate’, the provider of the proof a ‘prover’, and the computer that checks or verifies the
proof a ‘verifier’. In QMA, the setup is similar, except that the prover (named Merlin) provides
a quantum state as the proof instead of a classical solution, and the verifier (named Arthur) is
a quantum computer that checks the proof (in polynomial time), rather than a classical
computer. Or, to be more accurate, a quantum Turing machine rather than a deterministic
Turing machine.
P, NP and BQP are all considered to be part of QMA. With regard to P and NP, there
are problems that are known to be in QMA but not in NP, thereby making P and NP subsets of
QMA. However, it is possible that NP might actually be equal to QMA rather than being a
subset of it, i.e. it is not necessarily a proper subset. An example of a problem that is in QMA
but not currently known to be in NP is the Local Hamiltonian problem. This problem involves
determining the ground state energy of a quantum system, and it has been proven to be QMA-
complete (analogous to the class NP-complete). And just like there is a QMA-complete class
as an analogue to NP-complete, there is also a related concept of QMA-hard problems
(analogous to NP-hard), to which every problem in QMA can be reduced. In other words, the
Local Hamiltonian problem is as hard as any problem in QMA, making it a fundamental
problem in quantum complexity theory. In any case, while it is not currently known to be in
NP, if it does turn out to be in NP at some point, we will have QMA = NP and then the
relationship between P and QMA will depend on the P versus NP problem.
On the other hand, the rationale of BQP being a subset of QMA is that if a problem can
be solved in polynomial time using a quantum Turing machine, then a given solution for it can
certainly be verified in polynomial time as well, simply by comparison with the solution that
was just computed in polynomial time, just like the reasoning behind P being a subset of NP.
However, there could be problems that are verifiable by a quantum Turing machine in
polynomial time, but not solvable, i.e. being in QMA but not BQP, similar to the possibility of
P not being equal to NP.
Interestingly, there is also a variant of QMA known as QCMA (Quantum-Classical
Merlin Arthur), also known as MQA (Merlin Quantum Arthur), where the prover is restricted
to providing a classical proof, but the verifier still operates as a quantum computer. Recall that
in QMA, the prover provides a quantum proof to the quantum verifier, whereas in QCMA, the
prover is providing a classical proof to the quantum verifier. While this may seem like a minor
modification, it has a significant impact on the types of problems that fall within this class,
making it an interesting subject of study in its own right. QCMA is a subset of QMA because
classical proofs are less powerful than quantum proofs—if the prover sends a quantum proof,
the verifier can convert it into a classical one by immediately measuring it; therefore, QMA
contains QCMA.
In addition to BQP and QMA, another quantum complexity class we will briefly discuss
is QIP (Quantum Interactive Polynomial time). QIP is the quantum extension of a classical
complexity class known as IP (Interactive Polynomial time), which is actually equal to
PSPACE. Interactive proof systems are actually a full topic on their own, so we will not be
going into the detail of this class and just provide a brief overview. In classical interactive
proofs, a verifier interacts with a powerful prover through a series of exchanges. At the end of
the interaction, the verifier decides whether to accept or reject the prover’s claim. The key
point here is that the prover itself is computationally very powerful (essentially unlimited), but
the verifier is constrained in its computational power, so the interactions must take that into
account and still be able to convince the verifier of the prover’s claim. QIP adapts this
framework to the quantum domain, allowing for quantum interactions between the verifier and
the prover rather than just classical ones. By restricting the number of messages used in the
protocol to at most k, we get the complexity class QIP(k). Interestingly, QIP(0) is BQP and
QIP(1) is QMA. But probably the most interesting equivalence in this area is that QIP =
PSPACE, just like IP, meaning that the class of problems solvable by quantum interactive
proofs is equivalent to the class of problems solvable by a classical computer using polynomial
space. Thus, all of the quantum complexity classes that we discussed till now are also included
in PSPACE, just like the classical ones.
There are many other quantum complexity classes as well, though they are not as
widely discussed as the ones we have already covered. One such example is EQP (Exact
Quantum Polynomial time), which is the class of problems exactly solvable by a quantum
computer in polynomial time, i.e. without any error. This is in contrast to BQP, where we allow
a small probability of error. In EQP, quantum algorithms must produce the correct answer with
certainty. This means that at the end of the computation, the quantum state collapses into a
single state corresponding to the correct answer, rather than a probabilistic distribution over
several possible answers. One of the key examples of an algorithm that falls into EQP is the
Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm. This algorithm was one of the first quantum algorithms to
demonstrate quantum speedup over classical algorithms. It solves a specific decision problem
exactly, with no probability of error, and in polynomial time. The Deutsch-Jozsa problem
involves determining whether a given function is either constant or balanced (i.e., it produces
the same output for all inputs or produces an equal number of 0's and 1's). While a classical
algorithm would require multiple queries to the function to determine this, the Deutsch-Jozsa
algorithm can solve the problem using a single query in the quantum setting, providing an
oracle separation between EQP and P. This means that there are problems exactly solvable by
a quantum computer with fewer queries than a classical computer would require.
At first glance, it might seem that EQP should probably be a more important class than
BQP, as it provides exact answers rather than probabilistic ones. However, while it is good that
EQP deals with exact solutions, the fact that BQP allows for some error makes it a more flexible
and more widely applicable class. Many of the most well-known quantum algorithms, such as
Shor’s algorithm for integer factorization and Grover’s algorithm for unstructured search, fall
into BQP but not EQP because they produce correct results with high probability rather than
exact certainty. So, the class EQP is not as widely studied compared to BQP because many
quantum algorithms are more naturally probabilistic rather than exact. In any case, it is time to
conclude our discussion, as the other quantum complexity classes are considerably more
obscure compared to the ones we have discussed, and would be better encountered in a full
course on the topic rather than an introductory essay.
Conclusion
Let us summarise what we have seen so far. The most important classical complexity
classes are P, NP and BPP. P contains problems that can be solved in polynomial time using a
deterministic Turing machine, NP contains ones that can be solved using a nondeterministic
Turing machine, whereas BPP contains problems that can be solved with high probability using
a probabilistic Turing machine. It has not yet been proven what the exact relationship between
P and NP is, though it is widely believed that P  NP. Similarly, the relations of BPP with P
and NP are also not yet known for sure. However, it is known that all of these lie within
PSPACE, which means that they require at most polynomial space. On the other hand, we have

also a quantum class QIP from the field of interactive proof systems, which is analogous to the
classical class IP, and QIP = IP = PSPACE. Lastly, there is QCMA, which is a subset of QMA,
and also EQP which has an oracle separation with P but is less widely studied.
The study of quantum complexity classes also raises fascinating questions about the
relationships between quantum and classical computing. While BQP is widely believed to
contain problems that cannot be efficiently solved by classical computers (such as integer
factorization), it remains an open problem whether P = BQP, BPP = BQP, or whether these
classes are entirely distinct. These relationships are central to the broader understanding of
quantum computation and its potential to revolutionize fields like cryptography, optimization,
and machine learning.
In summary, quantum complexity theory provides a structured framework to classify
problems based on their solvability using quantum computation. BQP and QMA are the two
most prominent quantum complexity classes, each shedding light on different aspects of
quantum computational power. BQP captures the power of quantum algorithms in solving
decision problems with bounded error in polynomial time, while QMA highlights the ability
to verify quantum proofs in polynomial time. Beyond these, other classes like QCMA and QIP
extend the reach of quantum complexity theory, drawing important connections between
quantum and classical computational frameworks. As quantum computing continues to
advance, further exploration of these complexity classes will play a crucial role in
understanding the true capabilities of quantum machines. We hope that you enjoyed this essay
